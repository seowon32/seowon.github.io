Overview

Collaboratively designed and manufactured B.E.V.O., a competition-ready combat robot for the SPARC beetleweight tournament (3 lb class). The project integrated offensive weapon systems, structural durability, mobility control, and electrical safety into a lightweight platform designed to survive high-energy impacts while delivering mechanical damage and maintaining operational reliability.

Problem

Combat robotics is a multi-objective engineering challenge: maximize offensive effectiveness and maneuverability while meeting strict weight limits, safety regulations, and durability requirements. Our team needed to design a rule-compliant robot capable of inflicting damage, maintaining control, and remaining functional under aggressive combat conditions.

Engineering Objectives

  • Stay within the beetleweight (3 lb) class weight limit
  • Deliver effective weapon-based damage while maintaining control and aggression
  • Maintain mobility even if flipped during combat
  • Provide fast acceleration, stable driving, and tight turning capability
  • Meet SPARC safety requirements (shutdown, weapon locking, battery safety)
  • Balance performance with manufacturability and budget constraints

Design Innovation

The robot used a 2-wheeled wedge saw combat system that combined a lifting wedge with a rotating slicing weapon. The wedge helped elevate and expose vulnerable underside components of opponent robots, enabling the weapon to apply damage more effectively while preserving agility and control.

Engineering Approach

  1. Reviewed competition rules, judging criteria, and safety constraints
  2. Generated multiple concepts and evaluated trade-offs using structured decision methods
  3. Built CAD models and integrated subsystems into a manufacturable architecture
  4. Prototyped components and iterated based on functional testing and reliability risks
  5. Validated performance through competition-style testing and post-match analysis

Results & Impact

  • Produced a rule-compliant combat robot capable of offensive engagement and controlled driving
  • Demonstrated effective wedge + weapon engagement strategy during competition
  • Maintained drivetrain and electrical functionality during combat operation
  • Reduced risk priority score through reliability improvements (FMEA-driven iteration)

Failure Analysis & Lessons Learned

Post-competition evaluation highlighted trade-offs between weight and durability. Under repeated high-energy impacts, structural components showed damage consistent with material limitations, reinforcing the importance of material selection, armor strategy, and load-path design in dynamic systems.

  • Identified durability bottlenecks and stress concentration areas for future reinforcement
  • Improved understanding of how manufacturing choices affect reliability under impact loading
  • Outlined next-iteration improvements (materials, reinforcement, and modular replacement strategy)
