Overview

Designed and manufactured a SPARC-compliant combat robot focused on maximizing damage output, maneuverability, and survivability under strict weight and cost constraints. The final design integrated a dual-weapon wedge-saw system supported by structured engineering decision tools and iterative prototyping.

Design Challenge

Performance Goals
  • Inflict significant structural damage to opponents
  • Maintain mobility when flipped
  • Balance durability with strict weight limits
  • Remain within $300 cost budget
Competition Constraints
  • 3 lb maximum weight
  • Weapon shutdown safety requirements
  • Manual electrical disconnect
  • Restricted weapon types

Engineering Design Process

  • Voice of Customer derived from SPARC scoring criteria
  • House of Quality mapped user needs to engineering specs
  • Morphological matrix generated weapon and mobility variants
  • Decision matrix selected final architecture

Structured engineering methods allowed objective evaluation of tradeoffs between innovation, cost, and maneuverability.

Concept Selection

Concept Variants Explored
  • Horizontal spinning bar
  • Egg beater spinner
  • Drone combat robot
  • 4-wheel flipper
  • Wedge-saw hybrid (final)
Final Selection Rationale
  • High innovation score
  • Effective multi-weapon capability
  • Strong control and aggression potential
  • Best weighted score in decision matrix
Prototype of BattleBot for Driving Ability

Mechanical Architecture

Weapon System
  • Vertical rotating saw blade
  • Actuated wedge lifting mechanism
  • Belt driven weapon transmission
  • Linear actuator for opponent positioning
Mobility System
  • Two-wheel pivot steering
  • Flip-resistant geometry
  • Protected internal electronics

Testing & DOE

I led a factorial Design of Experiments to quantify performance tradeoffs.

  • Variables tested: chassis material, wheel type, actuator integration
  • Measured lap time over 8-ft arena distance
  • JMP and regression analysis verified significance

Results showed rubber wheels and PLA chassis significantly improved speed performance while actuator influence was minimal.

Manufacturing Strategy (DFMA)

Manufacturing Optimization
  • Standardized fastener sizes reduced assembly complexity
  • Manual machining avoided CNC setup time
  • 3D printed structural components reduced cost
Reliability Improvements
  • Redesigned actuator pickup piece after FEA stress analysis
  • Weapon shaft collar clamping prevented slip failures
  • Throttle limiting reduced motor overheating risk

Competition Performance

  • Dominated first opponent via wedge-lift and saw attack
  • Disabled opponent drivetrain and wiring
  • Lost second round due to armor failure vs metal spinner

Despite structural damage, electronics and weapon systems remained operational throughout testing and competition.

Engineering Lessons Learned

  • Weight constraints strongly influence material selection
  • Innovation often trades off against durability
  • Subsystem modularity accelerates testing and iteration
  • Early DFMA decisions reduce build time and risk
