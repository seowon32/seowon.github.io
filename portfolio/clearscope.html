Overview

ClearCam’s ClearScope is a laparoscopic lens-cleaning device, but traditional simulators don’t realistically reproduce debris events (thin films of blood/fat/debris on the camera). Our goal was to create a realistic and reusable demonstration environment for both internal testing and customer-facing demos.

My contributions
  • Mechanical design decisions focused on modularity and usability
  • Design-for-manufacturing considerations (print segmentation, joinery, assembly)
  • Testing plan + success metrics for durability, portability, and usability
Engineering focus
  • Realistic surgical lighting environment (dark interior)
  • Trocar insertion + repeated “debris event” reproduction
  • Fast access to internal cavity for cleanup and reset

Problem & Requirements

Problem

ClearCam’s previous testing setups were not realistic enough for consistent lens contamination demonstrations, limiting both R&D validation and marketing impact.

Key requirements
  • Torso form factor and cavity access
  • Supports trocar insertion and multiple incision points
  • Reusable tissue layer (replaceable) + internal components removable
  • Debris method that creates a thin film and wipes clean quickly
  • Portability targets for both platforms
Targets (examples): torso dimensions ~50×30×15 cm and ~5 kg, and portable box concept designed for lighter, faster demo setup.

Two Solutions

1) Full-scale torso simulator (R&D testing)
  • Modular torso housing with a sliding door to block external light
  • Internal shelf system for organ placement + controlled debris interaction
  • Tissue-mimicking layer with a mesh hold-down for repeatability
2) Portable demo box (marketing)
  • Compact, collapsible structure for easy transport
  • Pin-joinery and structural pins for quick assembly
  • Single mesh/tissue layer to mimic the demo interaction

Torso Simulator Design

Architecture
  • Torso-shaped housing with internal cavity
  • Sliding door system to isolate interior lighting
  • Sliding shelf with flexible organ positioning (peg/dowel concept)
Tissue & debris interaction
  • Ballistic gel sheet for a “skin/tissue” layer
  • Mesh support to prevent sag during trocar insertion
  • Debris approach optimized for “thin film” contamination (mustard used as debris in demos)
Manufacturing approach: housing printed in multiple parts due to printer size limits and assembled (adhesive bonding), with sliding shelf/door mechanisms designed for repeat use.
Torso Simulator Prototype

Portable Demo Box Design

The portable demo box was designed as a customer-facing platform: fast assembly, easy transport, and consistent demonstration of lens contamination + cleaning behavior. The deliverable focused on CAD and technical drawings so it could be manufactured later for sales/marketing use.

Key design features
  • Collapsible panels with pin mechanisms for lock/unlock
  • Vertical structural pins to secure walls + mesh together
  • Single mesh supports tissue layer (consistent demo interaction)
Why this works for demos
  • Portable form factor for conferences and sales meetings
  • Quick setup supports repeated demos in a day
  • Transparent material direction (acrylic) improves audience visibility

Testing & Validation

Durability / joinery integrity
  • Cycle testing for sliding shelf and sliding door (repeat open/close operations)
  • Pass criteria: no significant deformation after repeated cycles
Portability + usability
  • Measured weight and dimensions vs thresholds
  • Timed setup and teardown workflows for ease of use
  • Qualitative surveys: realism and usability ratings
Engineering validation approach: FEA + fatigue modeling were used to confirm low deflection and low stress on the internal shelf under representative loads, plus structured testing plans for debris application and skin replacement.
FEA Testing of Torso Simulator

DFM / Manufacturing Notes

What I’d do for production readiness
  • Convert printed parts to molded/formed panels if scaling (reduce print time)
  • Standardize fasteners and define tolerances for smooth sliding mechanisms
  • Design surfaces to prevent lens scratches (soft liners where needed)
  • Hygiene-first geometry: reduce crevices and improve wipe-down access
Key tradeoffs managed
  • Realism vs portability (torso: realism; box: portability)
  • Structural rigidity vs low weight
  • Debris effectiveness vs easy cleaning/reset between demos
