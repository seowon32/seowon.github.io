RC Racing Car Engineering Design (CALAPS)

Mechanical Design & Systems Integration Engineer · UT Austin · Spring 2025
Disclosure: Academic project with personally created content.

Overview

Designed and built a competition RC race car by integrating drivetrain, chassis, steering, and electronics into a lightweight high-performance vehicle. The project emphasized mechanical subsystem optimization, manufacturability, and reliability under real track racing conditions.

Engineering Objectives

  • Optimize powertrain efficiency and torque delivery
  • Design structurally robust yet lightweight chassis
  • Develop stable steering system with predictable handling
  • Balance performance tradeoffs between acceleration, speed, and reliability

My Contributions

Mechanical System Design

  • Designed and refined drivetrain architecture including pulley ratio selection and belt tensioning strategy
  • Contributed to chassis structural layout optimizing stiffness-to-weight ratio
  • Supported subsystem packaging and integration across drivetrain, steering, and electronics

Manufacturing & Assembly

  • Supported fabrication and assembly of drivetrain and structural components
  • Applied Design for Manufacturing principles to improve assembly robustness
  • Implemented retention features preventing component loosening during high-speed operation

Engineering Validation

  • Conducted performance validation for acceleration, speed, braking, and handling
  • Analyzed failure modes including belt slip and steering stability limitations
  • Drove iterative mechanical design improvements based on test results

Powertrain Design

The vehicle utilized a belt-driven transmission to transfer motor torque to the drivetrain. During integration, pulley availability required ratio changes, introducing new tension and reliability challenges.

  • Redesigned belt tensioning to prevent slip during high torque conditions
  • Added retention hardware to maintain drivetrain stability during racing loads
  • Balanced torque multiplication with top-speed performance

Chassis & Structural Design

The chassis was additively manufactured using nylon to maximize strength while minimizing mass. Structural geometry was optimized to handle crash loads and vibration without excessive weight penalty.

  • Optimized chassis stiffness and load paths through CAD iteration
  • Selected nylon for impact resistance and manufacturability
  • Designed structural mounting points for subsystem integration

Steering & Vehicle Dynamics

Initial Ackermann steering concepts were simplified to parallel steering due to packaging constraints and fabrication complexity. Real-world testing revealed tradeoffs between theoretical steering geometry and physical steering limits.

  • Evaluated steering geometry effects on turning radius and stability
  • Identified compliance and mechanical tolerance as key performance drivers
  • Implemented mechanical reinforcement to maintain steering consistency

Performance Results

  • Top Speed: ~4.5 m/s
  • Acceleration: ~3 m/s²
  • Lap Time: 15 seconds
  • Maintained mechanical and electrical functionality throughout race event

Engineering Lessons

  • Manufacturing constraints strongly influence final design decisions
  • Drivetrain performance depends heavily on mechanical stability and tolerance control
  • Theoretical steering models must be validated through physical system testing
  • Subsystem integration is critical to overall vehicle performance
