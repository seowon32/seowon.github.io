Reverse Engineering: Philips Sonicare 4100

User-centered redesign with manufacturable improvements
Target roles: Mechanical Design / Product Design / Manufacturing (DFM, prototyping, validation)
Why this project matters

Daily-use products often fail not because performance is weak, but because small friction adds up. I reverse engineered a Sonicare 4100 to identify experience failures (mess, noise, comfort), translate them into engineering requirements, and propose changes that remain manufacturable under cost constraints.

TL;DR (for hiring managers)
  • VOC interviews → HOQ to rank engineering requirements
  • Focused on splatter/drip reduction without hurting cleaning performance
  • Prototype + DOE to validate tradeoffs
  • DFM-first recommendation (low part count, cleanable geometry)

Overview

Problem & Goals

What users told me (VOC)
  • Toothpaste splatter during operation
  • Dripping after brushing (countertop mess)
  • Noise/discomfort from vibration
  • Feature requests (e.g., tongue cleaning)

The key insight: these are “experience failures,” not core performance failures—yet they drive dissatisfaction.

Goals
  • Reduce splatter/drip without compromising cleaning
  • Keep design cleanable and hygienic
  • Minimize added part count + assembly steps
  • Stay within realistic materials/process choices

Teardown & Architecture

The teardown confirmed key subsystems: battery/charging coil, motor/drive, vibration transfer mechanism, housing/seals, and user interface. This guided where design changes could happen without heavy electronics rework.

Subsystem-level takeaways
  • Cleaning depends on efficient vibration energy transfer
  • Noise “fixes” risk damping vibration (hurts performance)
  • Splatter/drip can be addressed at the head interface
Where changes are feasible
  • Brush head interface / external geometry
  • Grip/ergonomics materials and surface features
  • Minor housing/stand interactions (storage/hygiene)

Requirements (HOQ) & Tradeoffs

Top engineering requirements (from HOQ)
  • Vibration frequency / cleaning effectiveness
  • Compact storage volume
  • Brush stiffness / comfort
  • Power efficiency & battery life
  • Safety & hygiene constraints
Key tradeoffs
  • Noise reduction vs vibration transfer
  • Mess reduction vs added geometry/cleanability
  • Attachment features vs part count & assembly

Decision principle: prioritize changes that reduce daily friction while preserving core performance.

Concepts & Selection

Concept A — Splash guard geometry
  • Controls splatter trajectory near the brush head
  • Designed to be easy to rinse and wipe clean
  • Minimal added parts
Concept B — Drip catcher attachment
  • Reduces dripping after use
  • Attachment approach avoids reworking internal systems
  • Designed for snap-fit / press-fit manufacturability
Concept C — Noise mitigation (careful)
  • Target rattles/fit issues rather than damp vibration
  • Avoid “sound barrier” designs that block energy transfer
Selection
  • Primary focus: splatter/drip reduction
  • Best VOC alignment + manufacturability + low risk
  • Preserves cleaning performance

Prototype

I built a quick prototype to validate the concept direction: reduce splatter/drip while keeping the user interaction simple and cleanable. The prototype targeted function-first validation (not final industrial design).

Prototype intent
  • Test whether guard/catcher geometry reduces mess
  • Check for weight/ergonomic impact
  • Identify hygiene/cleanability issues early
What changed after prototyping
  • Prioritized smooth surfaces and radii (easy cleaning)
  • Avoided crevices that trap residue
  • Moved toward flexible materials (TPU/silicone)

DOE Validation

I ran a simple 2×2 DOE to evaluate how two variables impact cleaning: bristle stiffness and adding a sound barrier. Cleaning was evaluated by remaining “debris” after a fixed brushing time.

DOE factors
  • Soft vs stiff bristles
  • With vs without sound barrier
  • Controlled time + consistent test method
Key result
  • Sound barrier significantly reduced cleaning effectiveness
  • Stiffer bristles also decreased cleaning performance in this setup
  • Recommendation: avoid designs that damp vibration transfer
Engineering takeaway: comfort/noise improvements must be validated—some “nice” changes can break the main function.

DFM / Manufacturing Notes

Manufacturable attachment approach
  • Material: TPU (injection) or silicone (overmold) for comfort + washability
  • Attachment: snap-fit / press-fit aligned to existing geometry
  • Geometry: smooth radii, minimal undercuts, no residue traps
  • Assembly: minimal parts; reduce steps; easy replacement
Reliability mindset (FMEA)
  • Heat + battery safety constraints
  • Housing/seal integrity (water ingress)
  • Brush wear and maintenance indicators

For the next iteration, I’d add cycle testing (snap-fit durability), rinse-time testing, and splash radius measurements.
